Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI)

What is ICSI?

Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI) is a highly specialized form of in vitro fertilization (IVF) used to treat severe cases of male infertility. This advanced technique involves the direct injection of a single sperm into an egg to facilitate fertilization.

How Does ICSI Work?
Ovarian Stimulation and Egg Retrieval:
  • The female partner undergoes controlled ovarian stimulation using hormonal medications to produce multiple eggs.
  • These eggs are then retrieved from the ovaries using a minimally invasive procedure known as follicular aspiration.
Sperm Collection and Preparation:
  • Sperm is collected from the male partner either through ejaculation or, in cases of severe male infertility, directly from the testes or epididymis via surgical procedures.
  • The collected sperm is then processed and prepared in the laboratory to isolate the healthiest and most motile sperm.
Microinjection of Sperm:
  • Under a high-powered microscope, a single sperm is carefully injected directly into the cytoplasm of a mature egg using a fine glass needle.
  • This precise procedure bypasses the natural barriers that may prevent the sperm from penetrating the egg.
Fertilization and Embryo Development:
  • The injected eggs are then cultured in a controlled laboratory environment to allow fertilization and early embryo development.
  • Embryos are monitored and assessed for quality and development over the next few days.
Embryo Transfer:
  • The best quality embryos are selected for transfer into the female partner's uterus.
  • This procedure is done using a thin catheter, and it is typically painless.
Pregnancy Testing and Monitoring:
  • A pregnancy test is conducted approximately two weeks after the embryo transfer.
  • If successful, the patient is monitored closely through early pregnancy to ensure proper development and health.
Who Can Benefit from ICSI?

ICSI is particularly beneficial for couples facing the following challenges:

  • Severe male factor infertility: such as low sperm count, poor sperm motility, or abnormal sperm morphology.
  • Previous failed fertilization attempts: with conventional IVF.
  • Azoospermia: (absence of sperm in ejaculate), where sperm is retrieved surgically.
  • Unexplained infertility: or low fertilization rates in prior IVF cycles.
